El Silencio No es una Opción: Por Qué las Organizaciones Deben Defender la #DEI

Jan 24, 2025

La justificación de que muchas organizaciones permanecen en silencio sobre cuestiones de diversidad, equidad e inclusión (DEI) es insuficiente dado el clima político actual. Necesitamos organizaciones dispuestas a abogar y luchar en nombre de todos sus miembros, especialmente los más desfavorecidos. Muchas organizaciones han fallado consistentemente en esto porque tienen miedo de ser censuradas. Una organización que tiene miedo de defender lo que es correcto no vale la pena ser miembro de ella, especialmente cuando los programas como DEI, LGBTQ+ y la representación de minorías están cada vez más amenazados.

Algunos argumentarían que estas organizaciones son apolíticas, pero como señala Elie Wiesel,

“Debemos tomar partido. La neutralidad ayuda al opresor, nunca a la víctima. El silencio alienta al atormentador, nunca al atormentado. A veces debemos interferir. Cuando las vidas humanas están en peligro, cuando la dignidad humana está en juego, las fronteras nacionales y las sensibilidades se vuelven irrelevantes. Dondequiera que los hombres y mujeres sean perseguidos por su raza, religión o puntos de vista políticos, ese lugar debe - en ese momento - convertirse en el centro del universo.”

Cuando la gente pregunta, “¿Qué iniciativas o sistemas de apoyo están en marcha para grupos minoritarios como negros, hispanos, LGBTQ+ y otras comunidades subrepresentadas? ¿Quiénes son los líderes o defensores detrás de estos esfuerzos y cómo podemos involucrarnos o aprender más?”

Con demasiada frecuencia, las organizaciones guardan silencio. No tienen respuesta. Y por esa razón, cualquier organización que permanezca en silencio es un fracaso y cobarde. Para remediar esto, las organizaciones deberían:

  1. Establecer Políticas Claras de DEI: Desarrollar e implementar políticas que promuevan la diversidad, la equidad y la inclusión.
  2. Crear Sistemas de Apoyo: Establecer iniciativas y sistemas de apoyo adaptados a las necesidades de los grupos minoritarios.
  3. Nombrar Defensores: Designar líderes o defensores responsables de impulsar los esfuerzos de DEI y representar a las comunidades subrepresentadas.
  4. Fomentar la Participación: Proporcionar vías claras para que los miembros se involucren en las iniciativas de DEI y aprendan más sobre los esfuerzos en curso.
  5. Promover la Transparencia: Comunicar regularmente a todos los miembros los objetivos, progresos y desafíos de DEI de la organización.

Al seguir estos pasos, las organizaciones pueden demostrar su compromiso de apoyar a todos los miembros y crear un entorno más inclusivo.

The rationale that many organizations remain silent on issues of diversity, equity, and inclusion (DEI) is insufficient given today’s political climate. We need organizations that are willing to advocate and fight on behalf of all their members, especially the most disenfranchised. Many organizations have consistently failed to do this because they are afraid of being censured. An organization that is afraid to stand up for what is right is not worth being a member of, especially when programs like DEI, LGBTQ+, and minority representation are increasingly under threat.

Some would argue that these organizations are apolitical, but as Elie Wiesel points out,

“We must take sides. Neutrality helps the oppressor, never the victim. Silence encourages the tormentor, never the tormented. Sometimes we must interfere. When human lives are endangered, when human dignity is in jeopardy, national borders and sensitivities become irrelevant. Wherever men and women are persecuted because of their race, religion, or political views, that place must - at that moment - become the center of the universe.”

When people ask, “What initiatives or support systems are in place for minority groups such as Blacks, Hispanics, LGBTQ+, and other underrepresented communities? Who are the leaders or advocates behind these efforts, and how can we get involved or learn more?”

Too often, organizations are silent. They have no answer. And for that reason, any organization that remains silent may be perceived as a failure and craven. To remedy this, organizations should:

  1. Establish Clear DEI Policies: Develop and implement policies that promote diversity, equity, and inclusion.
  2. Create Support Systems: Establish initiatives and support systems tailored to the needs of minority groups.
  3. Appoint Advocates: Designate leaders or advocates responsible for driving DEI efforts and representing underrepresented communities.
  4. Encourage Involvement: Provide clear pathways for members to get involved in DEI initiatives and learn more about ongoing efforts.
  5. Promote Transparency: Regularly communicate the organization’s DEI goals, progress, and challenges to all members.

By taking these steps, organizations can demonstrate their commitment to supporting all members and create a more inclusive environment.

Why DEI is Important

The following is a definition of DEI via AI:

Definition of Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion (DEI)

Diversity: Diversity refers to the presence of differences within a group or organization. These differences can include, but are not limited to, race, ethnicity, gender, sexual orientation, age, religion, disability, and socioeconomic status. Diversity aims to create an environment where various perspectives and experiences are represented and valued.

Equity: Equity involves ensuring fairness and justice by addressing systemic disadvantages and providing equal opportunities for all individuals. Unlike equality, which focuses on treating everyone the same, equity acknowledges that different people have different needs and circumstances, and aims to level the playing field by providing resources and support tailored to those needs.

Inclusion: Inclusion is the practice of ensuring that diverse individuals feel valued, respected, and fully integrated into a group or organization. It involves creating an environment where everyone has a sense of belonging and can fully participate and contribute. Inclusion goes beyond mere representation and focuses on the quality of interactions and experiences within a diverse setting.

Oh, bother. Can’t they get the study right?

Should you whip out a notebook or a laptop to take notes?

The answer is not so simple.

You will want to read the whole piece:

“when taking notes by hand, “it is impossible to write down everything, so students have to process the incoming information, summarize it, and link it to knowledge they already have.” That helps the “new information to stick better, resulting in better retention.” source

I have noticed increased memory in my own note-taking by hand, but the real value does seem to be in revisiting my notes and trying to “do something with them.” Having live-blogged events, I noticed that it was easy to switch my brain off and type. It’s harder when you handwrite your notes to summarize (.45), outline and organize (.84), concept mapping (.66) and do your best to put it in your own words. That’s why audio recording is helpful…you can listen to it again and again, taking notes, drawing concept maps, engaging in quadrant note-taking, etc.

The challenge is learning how to do that, and THEN, revisiting your notes 2-3 days later, then again seven days later, etc. to ensure long-term information retention. But my personal experience isn’t proof. We need scientific research for that.

On a Journey from Hypothesis (Evidence Poor) to Theory (Evidence Rich)

This image captures the journey from hypothesis to theory…the science is still coming in, so to speak, but it’s leaning towards the right. But that, again, is for the scientists to decide. And I don’t think we are there yet.

We’ll have to wait and see what more studies say. That study aside, consider:

In kids, studies show that tracing out ABCs, as opposed to typing them, leads to better and longer-lasting recognition and understanding of letters. Writing by hand also improves memory and recall of words, laying down the foundations of literacy and learning. In adults, taking notes by hand during a lecture, instead of typing, can lead to better conceptual understanding of material.

“There’s actually some very important things going on during the embodied experience of writing by hand,” says Ramesh Balasubramaniam, a neuroscientist at the University of California, Merced. “It has important cognitive benefits.” (source)
